OVERVIEW

Why CozyLink?

CozyLink is a student startup aimed to tackle the fragmented, inefficient, and often unreliable rental process that many young adults, specifically students, face. The app itself is meant to create centralization by enabling students to search and discover reliable rentals without needing to consult external platforms and connect with other verified users, including renters, landlords, and tenants.

As the founding designer, I designed the entire app from scratch and created our visual identity. My designs focus on the renter account since I didn‚Äôt get to the lister account before the project ended.

PROCESS

Desired Features

Gathering insights from our interviewees, I organized the features users mentioned wanting in our app and grouped them under key concepts. The dots below the gray sticky notes represent the frequency these features were mentioned.

Overall, users want more transparency on rentals, such as additional fees outside of the total rent and the ability to verify the landlord or lister of each property. Users are also looking for easier ways to ensure compatibility and connect with prospective roommates. Finally, they want to maximize the amount of in-app communication to avoid using multiple apps.

Creating a Design System

This was my first time creating an extensive design system. After selecting our brand color, Pine-60, which was used for the logo, I developed an extended palette. I followed Brad Frost‚Äôs Atomic Design to structure my workflow and drew inspiration from Ant Design.

Colors

I developed a neutral palette using warm grays to reflect our brand's cozy, clean, and fun values. I then introduced colors for avatars and identified the need for semantic colors. All colors meet WCAG contrast standards against our lightest and darkest neutrals.

Typography

I initially used both Sora and DM Sans but later opted for just DM Sans for a cleaner look. While experimenting with other font families, I did appreciate Figtree for its slimmer letters, which resulted in better line fit, but I ultimately chose DM Sans for its structured appearance and sharper numbers.

Icons

I used a combination of Lucide.dev, Heroicons, and custom icons.

Adding the Fun

I designed these avatars as part of the account creation experience to offer a customizable option to our users.

Although these avatars are primarily used for profile icons, there is potential to expand their use to enhance empty states, error messages, and loading screens, making them more engaging and delightful.

Home / Initial Wireframes

While wireframing, I struggled with understanding the app‚Äôs vision. The homepage aimed to link to the roommate finder, subleasing, and subletting pages. However, I found some key issues: the distinction between subleasing and subletting was unclear, and there was significant overlap between the roommates and landing pages.

I initially interpreted the roommate board as for users who had secured rentals and were seeking roommates, rather than those who formed housing groups before the search. Additionally, there was no clear way for users to access the roommate questionnaire or rental posting form.

Home / More Iterations

After a few iterations, I enhanced the title with additional information, added a toggle for Rental and Lister Details on the rental details page, and introduced features aligned with our user research, including an interactive map, an amenities list, and a Q&A board.

I moved the roommate profile to the lister details and added landlord and tenant information to address high-priority user needs for greater transparency. I also updated the fixed footer with options to request a roommate or schedule a tour.

‚ö†Ô∏è Overcoming a Major Roadblock: Redesigning for Diverse User Needs

Since the team aimed to combine listing and renting functionalities within a single account, I designed with this approach in mind. However, this solution fell short of addressing the diverse needs of our users. While my iterations improved in meeting renter expectations, they struggled to effectively cater to all user types‚Äîrenter, sublessor, and landlord‚Äîdue to the difficulty of consolidating multiple functionalities into one account.

The forms, like the roommate questionnaire and property listing form, served different purposes for various user types. Renters needed the questionnaire once, sublessors used the listing form occasionally, and landlords required it multiple times. As a result, placing these forms in a quick actions button or grouping them within one account proved inefficient.

üìù Revisiting my research

After revisiting my competitor analysis, I concluded that a complete reorganization of the app was necessary. All the 7 platforms I analyzed had separate renter/guest and lister/host accounts for a good reason, which I found out the hard way. As a result, I needed to separate landlord and renter functionalities to better address the unique needs and functionalities of each user type.

While I didn‚Äôt end up getting to work on the lister account, I concentrated on tailoring the renter account to better meet renter needs.

CozyLink Site Map

After having multiple discussions with my team regarding the restructure, I created a site map to organize our ideas and understand the interactions within the primary navigation. This new navigation includes: Home (defaulting to rental units with a toggle for roommates), Messages, Community üÜï, Starred, and Profile.

Unfortunately, I didn‚Äôt work on the Community section since it was added towards the end of the project. Since younger student renters often see living off-campus as a step toward adulthood, the Community page would have been valuable for helping them acclimate to their new city and connect with other young adults through interest groups and local events.

I also had limited time to work on the Profile page, which would have included a way for users to switch between rental and landlord/lister accounts, either at the bottom of the page or through a long-press.

Onboarding Flow

Right off the bat, I wanted to minimize the amount of steps it takes for a user to reach the home page where they can view rentals while still having enough information in the backend to provide them with a complete homepage.

At the team's request, I made the first screen an optional sign-up page. Since we likely wouldn't have enough listings on our app to create an Airbnb-like homepage with appealing non-location-based listings, I added a required location field to the onboarding screens.

After mapping out user flows for both new users (create an account or continue as a guest) and existing users, I developed detailed flows for each scenario. While creating an account can be cumbersome, it is essential for users who want to do more than just browse, share, or star properties.

Start Browsing in Just 4 Taps

Users can access the home page in 4 taps or 3 selections. While browsing rentals doesn‚Äôt require an account, setting up a roommate profile is necessary to access the Roommates page, ensuring only genuine seekers can view profiles and enhancing safety.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 2

How might we streamline and centralize the rental process for users?

Interviews revealed that users struggled with fragmented communication and discovery across multiple apps, leading to inefficiency and frustration. To address this, I incorporated extensive functionality and detailed information into our app to minimize the need for users to rely on external apps.

Rental Details / People

I created sections to display individuals who starred the property (potential roommates), current tenants, and the landlord. By consolidating all contacts on one page, users have direct access to everyone involved isn the rental process, eliminating the need to use multiple apps for communication‚Äîa major pain point highlighted in interviews.

Adding a Location to My Places

I also designed My Places, an interactive map that lets users add their locations and automatically calculates distances and commute times. These locations are applied globally, meaning entries made on one rental details page will appear on other rental pages as well, though they can be edited in the user profile.

Share a Property

Users can directly share properties within the app with users they have connected with rather than copying a link to share outside the app.

Star a Property

Users can star properties and organize them into custom folders, which can be shared with other users.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 3

How might we make the rental and roommate search more enjoyable for users?

Given that the housing process is often cumbersome and draining, my goal was to enhance the experience by integrating more fun and engaging elements. I decided to incorporate social media-like elements into the app such as:

  1. Adding a customizable roommate profile card section in addition to the more tedious roommate questionnaire as part of the Roommate Profile creation process
  2. Requesting to message other users to mimic friend requests

Changes to the Roommate Profile Card

First, I needed to make some critical changes to our existing roommate profile card, as it plays a vital role in the app. I had previously identified issues with its visual appeal and the content, which was heavily focused on logistics and failed to capture the user's personality and interests‚Äîkey factors in fostering connections that could turn a roommate into a friend

While working on this, I recognized an opportunity to expand the app's use beyond renters to also support students living on-campus who are seeking roommates, as finding a compatible roommate is challenging regardless of living situation.

Creating a Roommate Profile

While the Roommate Questionnaire was always a required step in building a roommate profile, I introduced the Roommate Card Builder to bridge the gap between the Roommate Card displayed on the Roommates page and what the full profile page could look like.

Although this adds an extra step to the process, it shifts additional questions from the questionnaire to the card builder, giving users more creative freedom to personalize how they present themselves to prospective roommates.

Sending a Message Request

Allows users to initiate a connection and express interest in interaction with a user. Fosters community engagement and creates a controlled way for users to make contact.

Responding to a Message Request

Allows users to manage their communication, ensuring they only respond to requests from individuals they are interested in or feel comfortable interacting with. It enhances privacy and safety by filtering who can start conversations with them.

LEARNINGS

Takeaways

  1. Don't strive for perfection: My younger self would have never thought I'd say this. However, our strive for perfection severely hindered our progress. We aimed to create a flawless product rather than focusing on a MVP and allowing user testing to guide the rest of the product. We should have prioritized key features and planned releases more effectively instead of trying to launch everything at once.
  2. Focus on the overall experience: I spent an excessive amount of time on minor design aesthetics rather than on the overall user experience. This led to missing important edge cases and interaction details during prototyping. It‚Äôs crucial to balance visual design with usability to ensure a comprehensive and functional user experience.
  3. Align on goals early: We often found ourselves misaligned on goals and lacked a comprehensive brainstorming session at the beginning of the project. Holding such a session would have helped us clarify objectives and set a unified direction. I would advocate for early goal alignment and collaborative planning in future projects.
  4. Invest in discovery and definition: I would allocate more time to the discovery and definition phases of a project. This includes interviewing both renters and landlords, developing detailed user flows, and creating wireframes before moving on to mid to high-fidelity designs. Thorough exploration and definition of user needs and workflows are essential for creating a well-rounded and effective design.
